# Professor Emphasis Rubric

Score each concept from evidence, then use the total only as a prioritization aid. Cite every nonzero signal.

| Signal | Points | Examples |
|---|---:|---|
| Explicit exam or importance cue | +4 | “This will be on the exam,” “You must know this” |
| Repeated across separate lectures/materials | +3 | Revisited in multiple sessions or documents |
| Assignment, quiz, review-sheet, or past-exam use | +3 | Students practiced or were assessed on it |
| Extended explanation or multiple examples | +2 | Meaningful class time, worked examples |
| Contrast, exception, misconception, or correction | +2 | “Do not confuse A with B,” corrected student error |
| Foundation for later concepts | +2 | Required to solve or explain downstream topics |
| Student question followed by substantial professor elaboration | +1 | Professor expands, reframes, or generalizes |
| Merely listed or mentioned once | +0.5 | Heading, definition, passing reference |
| Explicitly excluded from scope | Exclude | “Not on the exam” |

## Confidence

- **High:** multiple independent signals, or an explicit cue plus supporting instruction.
- **Medium:** one strong behavioral signal or several weaker signals.
- **Low:** isolated mention, incomplete transcript, uncertain OCR, or ambiguous scope.

Resolve ties by preferring concepts that support application, integration, or distinctions the professor repeatedly taught. Never fabricate precision: rounded weights and explained judgments are better than pseudo-scientific scores.
