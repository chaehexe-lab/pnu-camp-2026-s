# Question Design Guide

## Blueprint

Create a compact blueprint with concept, emphasis weight, learning demand, format, difficulty, question count, and source reference. Ensure the final questions match it.

Suggested difficulty mix when the user gives no preference:

- 30% foundational: identify, define, or perform a direct procedure;
- 50% standard: explain, compare, apply, or calculate in a familiar setting;
- 20% challenging: integrate concepts, diagnose errors, or apply in a new setting.

Adjust to known course level and professor style. Do not force this mix onto purely practical, mathematical, or essay-based exams.

## Construction rules

- Test one clear target per item unless integration is intentional.
- Match cognitive demand to what was taught and practiced.
- Make distractors plausible through documented misconceptions, not trivia or wordplay.
- Avoid “all/none of the above” unless the professor demonstrably uses them.
- Avoid accidental hints from grammar, option length, repeated terms, or another question.
- Specify rounding, units, assumptions, allowed references, and response length where relevant.
- For essays, state the expected scope and provide an analytic rubric.
- For calculations, verify the numeric result and meaningful intermediate steps.
- For case questions, include all facts required to answer.

## Verification checklist

For each item confirm:

1. The cited source supports both the premise and answer.
2. One defensible answer exists, or the rubric admits valid alternatives.
3. The wording tests knowledge rather than reading tricks.
4. Difficulty comes from reasoning, not missing information.
5. The item adds useful coverage instead of duplicating another.
